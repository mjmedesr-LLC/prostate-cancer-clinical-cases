# Case 01: Elevated PSA in a 65-Year-Old Male

## Patient History

**Demographics:**
- Age: 65 years
- Sex: Male
- Ethnicity: Caucasian
- Occupation: Retired accountant

**Presenting Complaint:**
The patient was referred by his primary care physician after routine screening revealed a prostate-specific antigen (PSA) level of 6.8 ng/mL. The patient is asymptomatic with no lower urinary tract symptoms, no hematuria, no bone pain, and no unexplained weight loss.

**Past Medical History:**
- Hypertension (well-controlled on lisinopril 10 mg daily)
- Hyperlipidemia (managed with atorvastatin 20 mg daily)
- Type 2 diabetes mellitus (diet-controlled, HbA1c 6.4%)
- No prior history of prostatitis or urinary tract infections

**Family History:**
- Father diagnosed with prostate cancer at age 72, treated with radical prostatectomy, currently alive at age 92
- No family history of breast or ovarian cancer

**Social History:**
- Former smoker (20 pack-years), quit 10 years ago
- Occasional alcohol use
- No illicit drug use
- Married, lives with spouse

**Review of Systems:**
- No dysuria, frequency, urgency, or nocturia
- No hematuria or hematospermia
- No bone pain or unexplained fractures
- No erectile dysfunction
- Constitutional: No fever, chills, weight loss, or fatigue

## Diagnostic Workup

**Digital Rectal Examination (DRE):**
- Prostate approximately 40 g, firm, with a palpable 1 cm nodule in the right lateral lobe. No induration or asymmetry beyond the nodule.

**Repeat PSA:**
- PSA confirmed at 7.2 ng/mL (6 weeks after initial measurement)
- Free PSA: 12% (values < 18% are associated with higher cancer risk)
- PSA velocity: 0.8 ng/mL/year

**Imaging:**
- Multiparametric MRI of the prostate: PI-RADS 4 lesion (1.2 cm) in the right peripheral zone at mid-gland. No extracapsular extension or seminal vesicle invasion detected. No suspicious lymph nodes.

**Prostate Biopsy:**
- MRI-targeted and systematic 12-core transrectal ultrasound-guided biopsy
- Targeted cores from right peripheral zone: Adenocarcinoma, Gleason score 3+4=7 (Grade Group 2), involving 3 of 4 targeted cores (10%, 25%, 40% core involvement)
- Systematic cores: One additional core from the right base with adenocarcinoma, Gleason 3+3=6 (Grade Group 1), 5% core involvement
- All remaining cores benign

**Staging Workup:**
- CT scan of abdomen and pelvis: No lymphadenopathy or distant metastases
- Bone scan: No evidence of osseous metastatic disease
- Clinical stage: T2a N0 M0

**Risk Stratification:**
- NCCN Favorable Intermediate-Risk prostate cancer
  - Gleason 3+4=7 (Grade Group 2)
  - PSA 7.2 ng/mL
  - Clinical stage T2a
  - Percentage of positive cores: 4/16 (25%)

## Treatment Options

**1. Active Surveillance**
- Appropriate for favorable intermediate-risk disease per NCCN guidelines, though close monitoring is essential
- Protocol: PSA every 3–6 months, DRE every 6–12 months, repeat MRI at 12 months, confirmatory biopsy within 12 months
- Consideration: Patient has a palpable nodule and PI-RADS 4 lesion, which warrants careful discussion of surveillance versus definitive treatment
- Advantages: Avoids treatment-related side effects; may not miss the window for curative treatment with close monitoring
- Disadvantages: Risk of disease progression; anxiety associated with monitoring; potential for missed opportunity for early definitive treatment

**2. Radical Prostatectomy (RP)**
- Open, laparoscopic, or robot-assisted approach
- Nerve-sparing technique may be considered given the unilateral, low-volume disease
- Pelvic lymph node dissection recommended given intermediate-risk status
- Advantages: Definitive local treatment; complete pathological staging; low risk of local recurrence with negative margins
- Disadvantages: Risk of urinary incontinence (5–15% at 12 months); risk of erectile dysfunction (20–50% with nerve-sparing); surgical recovery period of 4–6 weeks

**3. Radiation Therapy**
- **External Beam Radiation Therapy (EBRT):** Intensity-modulated radiation therapy (IMRT) or volumetric modulated arc therapy (VMAT) to 78–79.2 Gy in 39–44 fractions
- **Brachytherapy:** Low-dose rate (LDR) or high-dose rate (HDR) as monotherapy or boost
- For intermediate-risk disease, combination of EBRT with brachytherapy boost or short-course androgen deprivation therapy (ADT) for 4–6 months may improve outcomes
- Advantages: Non-surgical; outpatient treatment; comparable oncologic outcomes to surgery for intermediate-risk disease
- Disadvantages: Bowel and bladder irritation; erectile dysfunction (may be delayed onset); rectal toxicity; need for daily treatments over 4–8 weeks

**4. Focal Therapy (Investigational)**
- Options include high-intensity focused ultrasound (HIFU), cryoablation, or focal laser ablation
- May be considered in the context of clinical trials or for highly selected patients
- Advantages: Potential for reduced morbidity while treating the index lesion
- Disadvantages: Not yet standard of care for intermediate-risk disease; risk of untreated disease; limited long-term data

## Discussion

This case illustrates a common clinical scenario in prostate cancer management: a 65-year-old male with elevated PSA and favorable intermediate-risk prostate cancer. Several key learning points emerge:

**1. PSA Screening and Referral:**
Routine PSA screening identified this patient's cancer at an early, potentially curable stage. The decision to proceed with further workup was supported by the elevated PSA level, low free PSA percentage, and family history. The USPSTF recommends shared decision-making for PSA screening in men aged 55–69, emphasizing the importance of informed patient choice.

**2. Role of Multiparametric MRI:**
The mpMRI played a critical role in risk stratification and biopsy targeting. PI-RADS 4 indicated a high likelihood of clinically significant cancer, which guided the targeted biopsy approach. Current guidelines support the use of mpMRI before biopsy to improve detection of clinically significant prostate cancer while reducing overdiagnosis of indolent disease.

**3. Risk Stratification and Treatment Selection:**
The NCCN favorable intermediate-risk classification places this patient in a category where multiple treatment options are appropriate. The choice among active surveillance, surgery, and radiation therapy should be individualized based on patient preferences, comorbidities, and quality-of-life priorities. Multidisciplinary consultation involving urology, radiation oncology, and medical oncology is recommended.

**4. Shared Decision-Making:**
Given the patient's age (65 years), good functional status, and family history of prostate cancer, all definitive treatment options remain viable. The patient's values regarding urinary, sexual, and bowel function should be explicitly discussed. Active surveillance is a reasonable option given the favorable features, but the patient should understand the commitment to close monitoring and the possibility of needing definitive treatment in the future.

**5. Follow-Up Considerations:**
Regardless of the treatment chosen, long-term follow-up with serial PSA monitoring is essential. After radical prostatectomy, an undetectable PSA is expected, and any detectable PSA may indicate biochemical recurrence. After radiation therapy, a PSA nadir followed by a rising trend (using the Phoenix definition of PSA rise ≥ 2 ng/mL above nadir) signals potential recurrence.

**6. Genetic Considerations:**
Given the family history of prostate cancer in a first-degree relative, germline genetic testing may be considered, particularly if there is a family history of other cancers (breast, ovarian, pancreatic) suggesting potential BRCA1/2 or other DNA repair gene mutations. This information could influence treatment decisions, particularly regarding the potential role of PARP inhibitors in advanced disease.
